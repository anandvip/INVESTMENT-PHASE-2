On the Things I Cannot Change

A meditation, left forward in time
Vipul Anand · 2026

If you have found this, you are descended from me, or you are a stranger who has stumbled into a record that was not addressed to you specifically. Either way, welcome. What follows is a thread I pulled on for an afternoon and decided to leave behind, in case it is useful where you are. It will not be. And then, one day, it might be.

· · ·

The sentence

I came across a sentence while wandering online, the way one wanders the bazaar of a foreign city without intending to buy anything.

I try not to let things I can't change bother me.

Something in it landed. The vibration had depth, as some words do when they meet you at the right hour. I sat with it. What follows is what I found there — not as instruction, but as a record of one mind walking around one sentence.

· · ·

The honest word

The honest word in that sentence is try. Not I don't — that would be a lie. Not I never — that is impossible for any creature with a nervous system. Try admits the practice. It confesses that this is daily work, not a station achieved.

Most spiritual sentences fail at this seam. They pretend to a state instead of a discipline. The sentence I found does not pretend. That is what made it land.

· · ·

Where the sentence quietly leaks

And yet. There are at least three places where this sentence can fail without the speaker noticing.

The first is that the boundary is leaky. Things I cannot change sounds objective, but in practice it slides into things I would rather not try to change. The category is self-serving by default. A useful test: would a wiser version of you, looking at the same situation, agree it is truly unchangeable — or notice an angle you have quietly written off?

The second is that bother is sometimes the signal, not the noise. Grief bothers you. Injustice bothers you. A friend's slow self-destruction bothers you. None of these are things you can directly change, but the bother is information — it tells you what you care about and where your values actually live. Mute the bother and you mute the compass.

The third is that the sentence tells you what to stop, but not what to start. The energy you reclaim has to go somewhere. If it just becomes numbness, that is bypass with a calm face. The Vedantic teachers were sharper than this: act fully on what is yours to act on, release the fruits, but feel everything that arrives. Detach from outcome, not from life.

· · ·

Why we drift toward the dismissal

This is the question that troubled me most. Why does the leak go in that particular direction — toward declaring more things unchangeable than truly are?

Some of the reasons are quietly biological. The brain is a metabolic miser. Closing a question costs less energy than keeping it open. I can't is the cheapest closure available. We are also, by measurement, roughly twice as sensitive to losses as to gains; declaring something unchangeable forecloses the risk of trying and failing. And a brain that minimises prediction error tends to confirm its model rather than update it; if the current model says this is fixed, actually testing whether it is would mean opening that model to revision, which is expensive.

But the deepest reason is older than biology. The older traditions called it avidya — active mis-knowing. Admitting I could change this but will not indicts the self. Admitting I cannot preserves the self-image. The cannot is doing protective work, quietly, every day, for the coherence of who you take yourself to be.

· · ·

The opposite failure

Here is something the sentence does not say, and that you must hold alongside it.

Humans do not have only one failure mode. We have two, and they run roughly opposite to each other. We over-engage with the unchangeable — rumination, regret, anxiety, grief that will not release, the mind chewing on what is already done or genuinely beyond reach. And we dismiss the changeable — avoidance, learned helplessness, can't, the mind closing the file on what is still open.

The sentence I found is medicine for the first failure. It is poison for the second. Same words. Two opposite ailments. Whoever reads such a sentence must know which they are bringing to it.

· · ·

The third clause

An old prayer in the West has three clauses: acceptance for what cannot be changed, courage for what can, and the wisdom to tell them apart. The first two are easy to recite. The third is unfinished homework, every time. It has to be done freshly in each new situation, with each new person, in each new hour, with no one to hand you the answer.

I came to believe that whenever a teaching has a third clause that sounds like an afterthought, that clause is almost always where the real teaching lives.

· · ·

In a household

This is where the framework earns its keep, and where it is most often abused: in the close quarters of family.

The line that matters is the line between trait and behaviour. Temperament, attachment style, the deep aesthetic shape of a person — those you cannot change in another, and trying is a form of harm. But how someone speaks to you, whether they show up, whether they repair after rupture — those are not traits. Calling those unchangeable is enabling, dressed in wisdom's clothes.

A working test I came to trust: would this person, given different incentives or different feedback, behave differently? If yes, it is behaviour, and accepting it is enabling. If no, it is trait, and accepting it is love.

And one more thing, because it is too easy to forget. Real acceptance is active. You still name what you see. You still ask for what you need. You still leave if you must. Fake acceptance looks like peace from outside but is quietly contemptuous from inside. The people who announce I have just accepted them with a small sigh are usually performing the second kind. The people who have actually accepted someone do not announce it. They simply keep showing up clearly.

· · ·

What my father did

My father had a way of warding off my mother's rough moods. When I was younger I called it his uniqueness. I see now it was active atmospheric work — absorbing weather so the room remained habitable. People who do this well usually pay for it somewhere quieter, in places no one else sees. He paid in his own way, I imagine. I did not see clearly enough at the time to ask him how he carried it.

I now sit in his seat with my mother, who is eighty-six and has not changed and will not change. The cannot change has slid into trait in the truest sense. The framework is not being abused here. It is being applied honestly. The interesting question is not whether her patterns will shift — they will not — but what my version of warding-off looks like, and what it costs me. His way was his. Mine does not have to be a copy of it.

If you carry a similar inheritance: do not copy the elder's exact method. Inherit the willingness, not the form. The form must always be your own.

· · ·

The silent treaty

In a working family, everyone eventually learns everyone else's cannot change zones. This is not a sign of failure. It is the silent treaty that makes households habitable across generations and across temperaments. The dysfunction lives in homes where those zones are denied — where everyone keeps trying to renovate everyone else into a different person.

If the air in your home shakes sometimes, this is not a problem. Households where the air never shakes are usually households where someone has gone numb, or someone is being managed into compliance. A little weather is a sign the room is alive.

· · ·

The shaking is the life

A household that never shakes is going anaerobic. So is everything else.

Soil that is never turned compacts and goes airless. Trees that never bend in storm grow brittle and snap in the first real one. Birds whose feathers never wet lose the oil that keeps them airborne. The freshness requires the disturbance. They are not two things. They are one thing seen at two different moments.

What corrodes is not the weather. It is the held weather — the grudge, the storage of storm as resentment after the sky has cleared. Weather is natural. Held weather is what kills the house.

I wrote that to myself as much as to you.

· · ·

What I leave you with

If a sentence resonates immediately, examine it twice. Once for what is true in it. Once for the failure mode it might be quietly enabling.

The wisdom in any teaching is rarely in its first or second clause. It is almost always in the third — the one that asks you to discriminate, in the moment, in your specific life, with no one to hand you the answer.

Acceptance is not the absence of weather. It is refusing to store the weather as grudge.

And — you are descended from people who tried. Try is the most honest verb. Inherit it.
